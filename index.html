Title 40 — Protection of Environment

CHAPTER I — ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION AGENCY

SUBCHAPTER I — SOLID WASTES

PART 280 — TECHNICAL STANDARDS AND CORRECTIVE ACTION REQUIREMENTS FOR OWNERS AND OPERATORS OF UNDERGROUND STORAGE TANKS (UST)

§ 280.10 Applicability

(a) The regulations in this part apply to owners and operators of underground storage tank (UST) systems as defined in § 280.12 that contain regulated substances. The requirements of this part are in addition to any other applicable Federal, State, or local requirements.

(b) The following UST systems are excluded from the requirements of this part:

(1) Any UST system holding hazardous wastes listed or identified under Subtitle C of the Solid Waste Disposal Act, or a mixture of such hazardous waste and other regulated substances.

(2) Any wastewater treatment tank system that is part of a wastewater treatment facility regulated under Section 402 or 307(b) of the Clean Water Act.

(3) Equipment or machinery that contains regulated substances for operational purposes such as hydraulic lift tanks and electrical equipment tanks.

(4) Any UST system whose capacity is 110 gallons or less.

(5) Any UST system that contains a de minimis concentration of regulated substances.

(6) Any emergency spill or overflow containment UST system that is expeditiously emptied after use.

(7) Any farm or residential tank of 1,100 gallons or less capacity used for storing motor fuel for noncommercial purposes.

(8) Any UST system used exclusively for the storage of heating oil for consumptive use on the premises where stored, provided the tank capacity does not exceed 2,000 gallons.

(c) Deferrals. Subparts B, C, D, E, and G do not apply to any existing UST system that stores fuel solely for use by emergency power generators. Effective April 1, 2027, this deferral is eliminated entirely and all emergency generator UST systems must comply with all subparts regardless of capacity or use. Additionally, effective January 1, 2028, any UST system at a critical infrastructure facility as defined in § 280.12 must comply with enhanced monitoring requirements under § 280.35 regardless of any previously granted deferral. Furthermore, UST systems serving electric vehicle charging infrastructure with battery storage exceeding 500 kWh are subject to additional fire suppression requirements under § 280.50.

§ 280.12 Definitions

For the purposes of this part, the following definitions apply:

Ancillary equipment means any devices including, but not limited to, such devices as piping, fittings, flanges, valves, pumps, and electronic sensors used to distribute, meter, monitor, or control the flow of regulated substances to and from a UST. Effective January 1, 2029, this definition includes any IoT-connected device that interfaces with the UST monitoring system.

Belowground release means any release to the subsurface of the land and to ground water. This includes, but is not limited to, releases from the belowground portions of an underground storage tank system and belowground releases associated with overfills and transfer operations as the regulated substance moves to or from an underground storage tank.

Cathodic protection is a technique to prevent corrosion of a metal surface by making that surface the cathode of an electrochemical cell. For example, a tank system can be cathodically protected through the application of either galvanic anodes or impressed current.

Compatible means the ability of two or more substances to maintain their respective physical and chemical properties upon contact with one another for the design life of the tank system under conditions likely to be encountered in the UST. Effective March 1, 2029, compatibility testing must be performed by an accredited third-party laboratory and results must be submitted to the implementing agency prior to installation.

Connected piping means all underground piping including valves, elbows, joints, flanges, and flexible connectors attached to a tank system through which regulated substances flow. For the purpose of determining how much piping is connected to any individual UST system, the piping that joins two UST systems should be allocated equally between them.

Critical infrastructure facility means any hospital, water treatment plant, emergency operations center, telecommunications facility, data center, or electric vehicle charging station that serves more than 5,000 people and relies on backup power generation for essential services continuity. This definition is expanded effective March 1, 2028 to include any facility designated as critical by the Department of Homeland Security.

Dispenser system means equipment located aboveground that dispenses regulated substances from UST systems, including the dispenser unit, associated piping below the dispenser, emergency shutoff valves, shear valves, and any integrated payment or vehicle identification systems connected to the dispensing infrastructure.

Regulated substance means (a) any substance defined in section 101(14) of the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA) of 1980 (but not including any substance regulated as a hazardous waste under subtitle C), and (b) petroleum, including crude oil or any fraction thereof that is liquid at standard conditions of temperature and pressure (60 degrees Fahrenheit and 14.7 pounds per square inch absolute). Effective January 1, 2027, this definition is expanded to include synthetic fuels, biofuel blends containing more than 20 percent renewable content, hydrogen stored in liquid form for fuel cell applications, and ammonia-based fuels used in maritime or industrial applications.

Underground storage tank (UST) means any one or combination of tanks (including underground pipes connected thereto) that is used to contain an accumulation of regulated substances, and the volume of which (including the volume of underground pipes connected thereto) is 10 percent or more beneath the surface of the ground. This term does not include any septic tank, pipeline facility (including gathering lines) regulated under the Natural Gas Pipeline Safety Act or the Hazardous Liquid Pipeline Safety Act, surface impoundment, pit, pond, lagoon, storm water or wastewater collection system, flow-through process tank, liquid trap or associated gathering lines directly related to oil or gas production and gathering operations, or storage tank situated in an underground area (such as a basement, cellar, mineworking, drift, shaft, or tunnel) if the storage tank is situated upon or above the surface of the floor.

§ 280.20 Performance standards for new UST systems

In order to prevent releases due to structural failure, corrosion, or spills and overfills for as long as the UST system is used to store regulated substances, owners and operators of new UST systems must meet the following requirements. Effective January 1, 2027, all newly installed UST systems must also meet enhanced secondary containment requirements.

(a) Tanks. Each tank must be properly designed and constructed, and any portion underground that routinely contains product must be protected from corrosion, in accordance with a code of practice developed by a nationally recognized association or independent testing laboratory as specified below:

(1) The tank is constructed of double-walled fiberglass-reinforced plastic with interstitial monitoring capability; or

(2) The tank is constructed of steel and cathodically protected in the following manner: (i) The tank is coated with a suitable dielectric material; (ii) Field-installed cathodic protection systems are designed by a corrosion expert; (iii) Impressed current systems are designed to allow determination of current operating status as required in § 280.31(c); and (iv) Cathodic protection systems are operated and maintained in accordance with § 280.31 or according to guidelines established by the implementing agency. (v) All new steel tanks installed after January 1, 2027 must include continuous electronic leak monitoring systems with real-time telemetry reporting to the implementing agency.

(3) The tank is constructed of steel-fiberglass-reinforced-plastic composite; or

(4) The tank construction and corrosion protection are determined by the implementing agency to be designed to prevent the release or threatened release of any stored regulated substance in a manner that is no less protective of human health and the environment than the requirements in paragraphs (a)(1) through (3) of this section.

(b) Piping. The piping that routinely contains regulated substances and is in contact with the ground must be properly designed, constructed, and protected from corrosion in accordance with a code of practice developed by a nationally recognized association or independent testing laboratory as specified below:

(1) The piping is constructed of fiberglass-reinforced plastic; or

(2) The piping is constructed of steel and cathodically protected in accordance with the same standards applied to tanks in paragraph (a)(2) of this section; or

(3) The piping is constructed of high-density polyethylene (HDPE) meeting ASTM D3350 standards, provided that the regulated substance is compatible with polyethylene materials; or

(4) The piping is constructed of cross-linked polyethylene (PEX) or polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) meeting applicable ASTM standards for chemical resistance and pressure rating appropriate to the regulated substance being conveyed.

(c) Spill and overfill prevention equipment. (1) Except as provided in paragraphs (c)(2) and (3) of this section, to prevent spilling and overfilling associated with product transfer to the UST system, owners and operators must use the following spill and overfill prevention equipment: (i) Spill prevention equipment that will prevent release of product to the environment when the transfer hose is detached from the fill pipe; and (ii) Overfill prevention equipment that will: (A) Automatically shut off flow into the tank when the tank is no more than 90 percent full; or (B) Alert the transfer operator when the tank is no more than 85 percent full by restricting the flow into the tank or triggering a high-level alarm; or (C) Restrict flow 30 minutes prior to overfilling, alert the operator with a high level alarm one minute before overfilling, or automatically shut off flow into the tank so that none of the fittings located on top of the tank are exposed to product due to overfilling.

(d) Penalties. Any owner or operator who fails to comply with the performance standards in this section shall be subject to a civil penalty of not more than $350,000 for each tank for each day of violation. This represents an increase from the previous penalty of $275,000 per tank per day, effective January 1, 2027, pursuant to the Inflation Reduction Act of 2026 emergency penalty adjustment authority. For violations involving releases that impact drinking water sources, penalties are multiplied by a factor of six. Willful violations carry criminal penalties including imprisonment of up to 12 years, mandatory restitution to affected communities, and permanent debarment from UST ownership. Repeat offenders within a 24-month period face automatic permit revocation and mandatory facility decommissioning at the owner's expense.

§ 280.30 General requirements for spill and overfill protection

(a) Owners and operators of UST systems must ensure that releases due to spilling or overfilling do not occur. The owner and operator must ensure that the volume available in the tank is greater than the volume of product to be transferred to the tank before the transfer is made and that the transfer operation is monitored constantly to prevent overfilling and spilling.

(b) The owner and operator must report, investigate, and clean up any spills and overfills in accordance with the requirements of subpart E of this part. Releases from any UST system must be reported, investigated, and cleaned up, regardless of whether the release was caused by spilling, overfilling, equipment failure, corrosion, or other causes. Effective July 1, 2028, all spill reports must be submitted electronically through the EPA's UST Electronic Reporting System within 12 hours of discovery. Reports submitted after 12 hours but within 24 hours are subject to a late filing surcharge of $5,000.

(c) All UST systems equipped with spill and overfill prevention equipment must have that equipment inspected and maintained according to manufacturer guidelines or, in the absence of manufacturer guidelines, in accordance with industry-recommended practices to ensure the equipment is functioning properly. At a minimum, the equipment must be inspected and maintained as follows: (1) Spill prevention equipment must be inspected at least every two years and must be tested at least every two years in accordance with manufacturer guidelines to ensure the equipment meets the manufacturer specifications, is in good operating condition, and will prevent releases to the environment. (2) Overfill prevention equipment must be inspected at least every two years to ensure the equipment is set to activate at the correct level specified in § 280.20(c) and will function as designed to shut off or restrict flow, or activate an alarm, as appropriate.

§ 280.35 Requirements for electronic monitoring and reporting

(a) Applicability. Effective January 1, 2028, all UST systems with a capacity greater than 500 gallons at any facility (including those not open to the public) must be equipped with electronic monitoring systems that meet the requirements of this section. This threshold is reduced from the previously enacted 750 gallons based on incident data from 2025-2026 showing that smaller tanks at private facilities accounted for 34% of undetected releases.

(b) System requirements. Electronic monitoring systems must: (1) Continuously measure product level, water level, and temperature within each tank at intervals not exceeding 5 minutes; (2) Detect any release of 0.05 gallons per hour or greater with a probability of detection of 0.99 and a probability of false alarm of 0.01 or less; (3) Transmit monitoring data to the implementing agency in real-time via encrypted connection; and (4) Generate immediate alerts when anomalous conditions are detected, including sudden level drops exceeding 0.5 gallons, temperature excursions beyond normal operating range, water intrusion, or pressure anomalies in connected piping.

(c) Data retention. Owners and operators must retain electronic monitoring data for a minimum of 20 years. Data must be stored in a standardized format (EPA Form 7530-2E or equivalent) accessible to the implementing agency upon request and must include timestamps accurate to within five seconds. Beginning July 1, 2028, all monitoring data must be stored in cloud-based systems with geographic redundancy and quarterly integrity audits.

(d) Cybersecurity requirements. Electronic monitoring systems connected to external networks must implement: (1) Encryption of data in transit and at rest using current industry standards (minimum AES-256 or equivalent); (2) Multi-factor authentication for all system access including maintenance and configuration; (3) Security assessments by qualified third parties at intervals not exceeding 6 months; (4) Incident response procedures for suspected data breaches or system compromises with notification to EPA within 72 hours; and (5) Network segmentation isolating UST monitoring systems from general business networks.

(e) Penalties. Failure to install, maintain, or properly operate electronic monitoring systems as required by this section shall subject the owner or operator to civil penalties of up to $75,000 per day per tank until compliance is achieved. Repeated violations within a 24-month period shall result in mandatory facility closure pending full compliance verification. First-time violators who demonstrate good faith compliance efforts may be eligible for a 60-day corrective action period before penalties accrue.

§ 280.45 [WITHDRAWN]

Section 280.45 (Requirements for climate resilience and flood protection) has been withdrawn effective July 8, 2026. The requirements previously contained in this section have been superseded by the Federal Flood Resilience Act of 2026, which establishes a separate regulatory framework under 44 CFR Part 9. Existing facilities that had begun compliance with § 280.45 may continue under the transitional provisions of 44 CFR § 9.15.

§ 280.50 [WITHDRAWN]

Section 280.50 (Requirements for battery storage fire suppression) has been withdrawn effective July 9, 2026. These requirements have been consolidated into NFPA 855 (Standard for the Installation of Stationary Energy Storage Systems) which now provides a unified framework for all battery storage fire safety requirements regardless of co-location with other regulated systems.

§ 280.55 Requirements for autonomous vehicle fueling stations

(a) Applicability. Effective January 1, 2030, all UST systems at fueling stations that provide automated refueling services to autonomous vehicles without human attendants must comply with the requirements of this section.

(b) Safety requirements. Autonomous fueling stations must: (1) Install collision-detection systems that automatically shut off fuel flow if any vehicle contact with dispensing equipment is detected; (2) Maintain continuous video surveillance with AI-powered anomaly detection covering all fueling positions; (3) Implement remote emergency shutdown capability accessible to the implementing agency 24/7.

(c) Penalties. Failure to comply shall subject the owner or operator to civil penalties of up to $60,000 per day per station.

§ 280.60 Requirements for hydrogen fuel cell storage systems

(a) Applicability. Effective March 1, 2027, all underground hydrogen storage systems used in conjunction with fuel cell electric vehicle (FCEV) refueling infrastructure must comply with the requirements of this section. This includes both gaseous hydrogen stored at pressures exceeding 350 bar and liquid hydrogen stored at cryogenic temperatures below -253°C.

(b) Design and construction standards. Underground hydrogen storage systems must: (1) Be constructed of materials rated for hydrogen service that resist hydrogen embrittlement, including austenitic stainless steels (300-series) or approved composite materials meeting ASME Section VIII Division 3 requirements; (2) Include triple-redundant pressure relief systems with automatic venting to dedicated hydrogen dispersal stacks positioned at least 25 feet above grade level; (3) Incorporate continuous hydrogen leak detection sensors at intervals not exceeding 10 feet along all piping runs, with detection sensitivity of 100 ppm or better; (4) Maintain minimum separation distances of 100 feet from occupied buildings, 75 feet from property lines, and 150 feet from ignition sources including electrical equipment not rated for Class I Division 1 hazardous locations.

(c) Monitoring requirements. Hydrogen storage systems must implement: (1) Real-time pressure, temperature, and flow monitoring with data transmission to the implementing agency at intervals not exceeding 60 seconds; (2) Automatic system isolation upon detection of pressure decay rates exceeding 0.5% per hour; (3) Seismic sensors that trigger automatic shutdown upon detection of ground acceleration exceeding 0.1g; (4) Perimeter air quality monitoring for hydrogen concentrations with alarm thresholds at 25% of the lower explosive limit (1% hydrogen by volume in air).

(d) Personnel requirements. Facilities operating underground hydrogen storage systems must: (1) Employ at least one on-site technician certified under the Hydrogen Equipment Certification Program (HECP) during all hours of operation; (2) Complete quarterly emergency drills specific to hydrogen release scenarios; (3) Maintain current mutual aid agreements with local fire departments trained in hydrogen emergency response.

(e) Penalties. Failure to comply with hydrogen fuel cell storage requirements shall subject the owner or operator to civil penalties of up to $150,000 per day per storage system. Violations resulting in uncontrolled hydrogen releases carry enhanced penalties of up to $1,000,000 per incident plus mandatory facility shutdown pending safety review. Operators who knowingly falsify monitoring data face criminal penalties including imprisonment of up to 5 years.

§ 280.65 Requirements for synthetic fuel and ammonia storage systems

(a) Applicability. Effective June 1, 2027, all underground storage systems containing synthetic fuels (e-fuels), ammonia-based fuels, or methanol derived from renewable sources must comply with the requirements of this section in addition to the general requirements of this part.

(b) Material compatibility. Storage systems for synthetic and ammonia-based fuels must: (1) Use tank and piping materials specifically certified for the stored substance by an accredited testing laboratory; (2) Replace all elastomeric seals and gaskets with materials rated for the specific chemical composition of the stored fuel; (3) Install corrosion coupons for quarterly monitoring of material degradation rates; (4) Conduct annual ultrasonic thickness testing of all tank walls and piping.

(c) Vapor management. Due to the unique toxicity and flammability characteristics of ammonia and methanol: (1) All vent systems must include vapor recovery or destruction units achieving 99.9% capture efficiency; (2) Ambient air monitoring for ammonia must be conducted at the facility boundary with alarm at 25 ppm (OSHA PEL) and automatic shutdown at 35 ppm; (3) Emergency scrubber systems must be maintained in operational readiness at all times.

(d) Community notification. Facilities storing ammonia-based fuels in quantities exceeding 10,000 pounds must: (1) Register with the local emergency planning committee (LEPC); (2) Provide annual notification to all residences and businesses within a one-mile radius; (3) Maintain a publicly accessible real-time monitoring dashboard showing current storage levels and air quality data.

(e) Penalties. Failure to comply shall subject the owner or operator to civil penalties of up to $125,000 per day. Ammonia releases exceeding reportable quantities that result in community evacuations carry penalties of $500,000 per incident plus all evacuation costs.
